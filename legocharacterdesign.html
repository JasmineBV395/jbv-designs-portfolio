LEGO Character Design

Made in Autodesk Maya

Background

Before graduating from Leeward Community College in Spring 2025, I took 3D Modeling and Animation as one of my final requirements. One of the assignments for the course was to create and model an original LEGO character as a way to learn the fundamentals of designing a 3D animated model.

Prior to Process

For this assignment, my class used Autodesk Maya. I was already familiar with placing and extruding objects, as well as creating simple animations such as squash-and-stretch, so building a LEGO character didn’t seem too challenging. However, I was not very familiar with the LEGO brand itself, as I hadn’t researched it much beforehand. Because of that, creating a unique design with a required character background proved to be twice as difficult.

Project Process

Research

Our instructor asked us to create an original LEGO character based on either an existing piece of media, one of LEGO’s own series, or a world of our own creation. Initially, after looking up existing LEGO brands, I planned to design a character based on The Invisible Man for the pre-existing LEGO series, Monster Fighters. However, due to time constraints, I decided it would be easier to create my own character and world.

My LEGO character is named Ambrosia Carter, an open-minded yet overthinking 26-year-old archaeologist living in a distant future blended with gothic fiction and Lovecraftian horror. Her goal is to reconstruct Earth’s lost history without succumbing to insanity from horrors beyond her comprehension, including hideous monsters that roam the world and ancient ruins that unsettlingly resemble hospitals or laboratories. Ambrosia carries a satchel and shovel, both for self-defense and her work.

Sketching

My instructor provided a LEGO character design template to help us fit our concepts into the proportions of a LEGO minifigure. Using the template, I focused on balancing simplicity with characterization through facial expressions and clothing suited to the nature and timeline of Ambrosia’s world.

Based on her background, Ambrosia mostly wears old-fashioned clothing: a rolled-up, puffy-sleeve blouse, tucked-in pants, gloves, boots, and a belt. She also wears glasses, a ribbon-brooch tie, and has dark circles under her eyes to emphasize her personality. I also sketched her shovel and satchel to resemble existing LEGO accessories, such as Indiana Jones’s satchel.

Modeling

Following the instructor’s materials, we constructed our LEGO figures by manipulating basic objects (cylinders, cubes, etc.), creating textures after UV mapping the surfaces, and applying a skeleton with painted joint weights to produce a functional 3D model for animation.

I began by modeling the base LEGO figure using simple object shapes, extruding surfaces, adding edge loops to match LEGO proportions, and adjusting vertices as needed. Because my character has custom hair and accessories, I had to model them from scratch based on my sketches and standard 3D modeling techniques. This part was challenging but enjoyable.

Moving forward, we had to get started on UV Mapping for each limb and accessory to prepare surfaces for texturing. We separated the UVs into flattened 2D shapes after applying a template UV map to prevent stretching. This made painting textures easier and helped identify which surfaces belonged to which parts (e.g., face, neck). Since it was my first time UV mapping, I struggled particularly with the hair and accessories, and my initial results weren’t perfect. However, the process taught me why UV mapping is essential and how it can ultimately save time in future 3D projects. Afterward, I applied my custom textures I designed in Procreate and made a few final adjustments.

Finalizing

Finally, we moved onto the Rigging stage, where we applied a skeleton, bound joints to the model, and painted skin weights so the mesh would deform correctly. Adding the skeleton and labeling the joints was straightforward, but weight painting required far more trial and error than I expected.

Weight painting determines how strongly each vertex follows each joint: white indicates full influence, while green, blue, and black represent decreasing influence. At first, I struggled to understand this, and I often had to restart or reapply the skeleton. At times, paint would glitch or apply incorrectly until I found alternative approaches. The satchel was especially difficult; I initially added multiple joints to make it behave like a real bag. However, my instructor later explained that one joint would have been enough and far more efficient.

In the end, I was able to move the model smoothly and experiment with body language and accessory movement.

Results

Despite the challenges and constant adjustments throughout the assignment, the experience was very rewarding. I received a good grade, but more importantly, the project taught me how to approach future 3D assignments more efficiently and creatively. Since 3D modeling and animation are subjects I hope to continue exploring for fun, this project will stay with me for a long time.
